Some history of Arizona

Arizona officially gained its statehood in 1912, becoming the 48th U.S. state, mainly focused on its farming and mining ability. However, in a tradition still noticable today older members of society began immigrating down to the state in 1945 appreciating the warmth and low costs at the time. Arizona originally has broken away from mexico, later becoming part of the states. But the signs of its history can be seen in many places out in the desert. Arizonas population was also known to fluctuate, as events like the california gold rush caused people to settle in the area, but later leave. This would result in many ghost towns being made. In the civil war, arizona chose to side with the confederate states, only to be soon retaken by the union only a year later.

When it comes to native americans, the starting relationship was hostile. The local native americans constantly attacked military forts and ranches in the area. However, the results that came from military operations against the indians would cause the "long walk", when in thousands of navajo indians were made to walk through the desert to an indan reservation. This would in turn, cause the deaths of hundreds of indians along the road, both intentonal and unintentional. However negotiations would later start with the indians and the relationship would gradually repair. The local native americans would later gradually return to their own territories, and reservations began to form around these areas, with some still around today. Indians would ultimately gain their citizenship in 1924, and many of the military forts built for the conflict against the indians were decomissioned, with some still standing to this day.

Arizonas history would ultimately end up as a state mainly around farming, and its copper mines. Today still an ever-important resource for the arizona and U.S. economy. In many locations mining towns can be found, filled with nothing, usually. But towns like the town of tombstone, a mining town with a mine that lasted several decades still exist, and are still populated. Arizona was also highly desirable to many ranchers coming from texas, and arizona was rapidly taken over by ranching. With cheap prices for lots of land most towns that weren't dedicated to mining were filled with ranching.
